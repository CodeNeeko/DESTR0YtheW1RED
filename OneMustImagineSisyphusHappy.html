One must imagine Sisyphus Happy

Background: Sisyphus was the first king of Ephyra. Although his kingdom flourished, he was an evil dictator. He had many tricks up his sleeve to deceive the gods, which eventually angered Zeus and the other gods when he outsmarted them and cheated death twice. They sentenced him to eternally push a massive boulder up a hill. Every time he neared the top, the boulder would roll all the way back down, forcing him to repeat the task for eternity.

"One must imagine Sisyphus happy" - Albert Camus

Why would he be happy with his punishment?
Sisyphus's scenario seemed familiar to Camus, who wrote about it in his book The Myth of Sisyphus, in which he delves into the idea of the absurd; from Camus's perspective, the conflict between the desire for meaning and the meaninglessness of the universe.
Just as Sisyphus's task lacks any ultimate purpose or end, Camus argued the same could be said of the human experience. But rather than running away from the absurd, Camus tells us to confront it directly. We should revolt against our absurd condition by embracing life, creating our own meaning, and living authentically and there we can find happiness.
Sisyphus has no concept of a better day or an afterlife; his experience is tied to the "here" and "now." Sisyphus embraces the task and gives it his all, revolting against despair. This allows him to be the master of his own story, he is free, he is the hero of the story, a tragic hero.
Camus imagined Sisyphus smiling as he descends the mountain to begin his task again.

How could we apply this in our own lives?
We accept the world around us as made up of rules created by someone else. For example, religion, which offers peace through the promise of an afterlife, or monetary gain, which offers peace through the promise of financial stability once we retire. But Camus rejected the idea that "one day we will be free." Instead, he argued we should recognize that we are free at every moment and that the heaven is on earth.
Live our lives as much as possible, rather than as good as possible.
